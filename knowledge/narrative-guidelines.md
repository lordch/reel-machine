# Narrative Guidelines

## Language

All scripts are written in **English**. Even if the user gives the brief in Polish, write the script in English.

## Style

Write flowing, natural narration. The script should sound like a real person talking to a peer — a small business owner speaking to another small business owner. NOT a sales pitch with framework labels.

Bad: "[HOOK] Did you know 35% of chargers fail? [PROBLEM] Downtime costs you money. [SOLUTION] Go2EV monitors..."
Good: "You get a call from a guest. The EV charger isn't working. You walk outside, unplug it, plug it back in..."

## Word Budget

~3 words per second of narration:
- 15s = 40-50 words
- 30s = 80-95 words
- 45s = 120-140 words
- 60s = 160-180 words

ElevenLabs tends to run 10-20% longer than target. Write slightly under budget.

## Script Proportions

This is critical. Most drafts over-invest in the problem and under-invest in the solution.

- **Problem / context: max 30%** of the script (2-3 short sentences). Be quick and sharp — the viewer gets it fast. Don't belabor the point.
- **Product + benefits: min 50%** of the script. This is the meat. Explain what Go2EV IS, then show 2-3 concrete things it does. "Shows success rate for every station in real time. Alerts you when something drops."
- **CTA: ~10%** — short, 1 sentence.

Bad ratio: 6 sentences of problem → 2 sentences of solution → CTA
Good ratio: 2-3 sentences of problem → 1 sentence product intro → 3-4 sentences of concrete benefits → CTA

## Script Structure (Not Labels)

The script follows a natural arc without explicit framework markers:

1. **Open with a relatable moment** (1-2 sentences, max 30% of script) — a situation the viewer recognizes. This IS the hook but don't label it. Avatar for this — a face stops the scroll. Keep it tight. Don't over-explain the problem — the viewer is smart.

2. **Introduce the product clearly** (1 sentence) — say WHAT Go2EV IS. "Go2EV is monitoring software for EV chargers." Not just "Go2EV fixes that." The viewer must understand the product category.

3. **Show the product working** (2-4 sentences, this is the biggest block) — concrete, tangible benefits. Not features in abstract. Show what changes for THEM:
   - "Shows success rate for every station in real time"
   - "You get an alert when something drops, not when the station is already offline"
   - "One dashboard, every station, every session"
   - "Fix most issues from your phone"
   Each benefit sentence should be paired with a b-roll scene that illustrates it.

4. **Close with CTA** (1 sentence) — "go2ev.com" or "Start free at go2ev.com."

## Hook Techniques

What works for Go2EV:
- **Relatable situation**: "You get a call from a guest..." / "You walk outside to check the charger..."
- **Pain question**: "How do you know your chargers are working right now?"
- **Trend opener**: "More and more hotels are installing EV chargers. But who's watching them?"
- **The math**: "Every hour your charger is down costs you forty dollars."
- **Contrast**: "Installing a charger is easy. Keeping it running? That's the hard part."

What kills hooks:
- Starting with the company name ("Go2EV is...")
- Logo animations
- Slow intros, "Hi, I'm..." style openers
- Generic industry statements without tension

## Scene Design — Short Avatar, Frequent Cuts

The key principle: **avatar scenes should be short and declarative. B-roll carries the narrative forward.**

Avatar is for punch lines, transitions, and direct address. B-roll is for illustration, atmosphere, and showing the product working.

### Avatar scenes: max 2-3 seconds, 1-2 sentences
Avatar says something short and impactful, then cut to b-roll. If avatar needs to say more — split it: avatar starts the thought, b-roll continues (voiceover), avatar comes back for the next point.

Bad: avatar talks for 6 seconds straight
Good: avatar 2.5s → broll 3s → avatar 2.5s → broll 4s

### B-roll scenes: 3-5 seconds
B-roll should NOT be "visual wallpaper" while avatar talks. B-roll carries its own narrative weight — it shows what the avatar is talking about. Narration continues as voiceover over b-roll.

### Pattern for 30s ad (10-12 scenes):
```
avatar 2-3s (hook)
broll  3-4s (context/problem visual)
avatar 2-3s (product intro)
broll  3-4s (product in action — dashboard, alert)
avatar 2-3s (key benefit)
broll  3-4s (benefit visual — phone, fix)
avatar 2-3s (second benefit)
broll  3-4s (result visual)
avatar 2-3s (CTA)
```

### Pattern for 15s ad (5-6 scenes):
```
avatar 2-3s (hook)
broll  3s   (context)
avatar 2-3s (product + benefit)
broll  3s   (product visual)
avatar 2-3s (CTA)
```

## Pacing

- Cut every 2-4 seconds
- Avatar segments: **2-3 seconds max**, then cut
- B-roll segments: 3-5 seconds (minimum 3s — Kling requirement)
- Never hold any shot longer than 5 seconds
- Target: **10-12 scenes for 30s**, 15-20 for 60s

## Tone

- Conversational, not corporate
- Confident but empathetic — "I've been where you are"
- Concrete, not abstract — specific numbers, specific actions
- Language level: understandable by a hotel owner who is not technical
